Genetic blueprint

Variants that affect your day-to-day health choices. Pharmacogenomic variants for drugs you're not taking and variants with no clinical consequence are omitted.

MTHFR — the folate processor
Pay attention
One copy of the variant · rs1801133 (C677T) · ~1 in 4 people have this
The simple version: Your body needs folate (vitamin B9) for thousands of jobs — repairing DNA, making brain chemicals, clearing toxins, building healthy blood cells. But folate from food and most vitamins comes in a "raw" form your body has to convert before it can use it. Your MTHFR gene makes the converter.
What's different about yours: Your converter runs at about 65% efficiency instead of 100%. It's not broken — it just works slower than average. About a quarter of people have this exact variant.
Why this matters for your blood: When folate doesn't get processed properly, a substance called homocysteine builds up. Elevated homocysteine damages blood vessel walls and contributes to plaque buildup. Your current homocysteine is 8.4 — above the target of 7. This is directly fixable.
The folic acid trap: Most cheap multivitamins, fortified breads, and cereals contain "folic acid" — the synthetic raw form. With your variant, that synthetic version can actually block the receptors your body needs. You want "methylfolate" or "5-MTHF" on the label — that's the pre-converted form your body can use immediately.
One thing to flag with doctors: If you're ever prescribed methotrexate (used for arthritis or some cancers), they need to know about this variant — it changes how the drug behaves. Same goes for some antidepressants.
Affects cardiovascular riskAvoid synthetic folic acidUse methylfolate insteadFlag with prescribing doctors
What to do: Take Thorne Methyl-Guard Plus daily — it has the right form of folate plus B12 and B6 to support the whole pathway. Read labels and avoid anything saying "folic acid." Get homocysteine retested in July — target is below 7. Avoid fortified breads and cereals as a daily staple.
CFH — the eye protector
Monitor closely
One copy of the variant · rs1061170 (Y402H) · affects macular degeneration risk
The simple version: Your eyes have their own immune system. The CFH gene controls inflammation in the retina — specifically in the macula, the small central area responsible for sharp vision. Think of it as the retina's fire suppression system.
What's different about yours: Your version of CFH is less effective at suppressing inflammation in the macula. Over decades, small amounts of uncontrolled inflammation accumulate and can damage the light-sensing cells there — the leading cause of vision loss in people over 60 in developed countries.
Why Texas matters here: UV exposure accelerates the damage this variant predisposes you to. You play golf outdoors in one of the sunniest states in the country. That's a reason to be consistent with sun protection — not a reason to panic.
The good news: This won't affect your vision for decades. The omega-3s you're already taking directly support retinal health. Starting annual eye exams now gives you a baseline that makes future changes detectable early — when they're still fully treatable.
Macular degeneration riskUV protection essentialAnnual eye exam requiredOmega-3s help
What to do: Book a dilated eye exam with an ophthalmologist this year — establish a macular baseline. Wear UV-blocking sunglasses every time you're outside, especially golfing. Never smoke. Continue EPA omega-3s — they directly support retinal health for this variant.
PNPLA3 — the liver vulnerability
High relevance
One copy of the variant · rs738409 (I148M) · strongest known genetic risk factor for fatty liver
The simple version: Your liver constantly processes fat — pulling it out of cells, repackaging it, sending it elsewhere. PNPLA3 is one of the enzymes that manages this. Your version is less efficient at clearing fat from liver cells.
What's different about yours: Fat accumulates in your liver cells more easily than average — especially when alcohol is involved. Alcohol floods the liver with fat it can't clear fast enough, and your PNPLA3 variant makes that bottleneck worse. This is the direct genetic explanation for your three episodes of elevated AST/ALT on record.
What this means practically: Your liver isn't broken — it has less margin for error. The same amount of alcohol that another person's liver handles without issue can tip yours into inflammation territory. Your sobriety protocol isn't just willpower — it's medically appropriate given this variant.
The NAT2 compounding factor: You also carry a fully homozygous NAT2 slow-acetylator genotype. Both variants affect liver detox capacity simultaneously — your liver is working harder than the average person's at baseline.

COMT — the stress dial
High relevance
One copy of the variant · rs4680 (Val158Met) · affects dopamine clearance speed
The simple version: After something stressful, exciting, or intense happens, your brain releases dopamine. The COMT enzyme clears it so you can return to baseline. Think of it as the brain's reset button after a big moment.
What's different about yours: Your reset button is slower. Dopamine lingers longer before being cleared. On good days this is genuinely useful — sharper focus, stronger drive, better performance under pressure. Under sustained stress, it means your system stays activated longer, anxiety accumulates faster, and you're harder to wind down.
Why caffeine hits you harder: Caffeine amplifies dopamine activity. Combined with slower clearance, an afternoon coffee keeps your brain running on high until well into the night. This is a direct mechanism behind your insomnia — it's not just habits, it's biochemistry.
The anxiety connection: Your GAD diagnosis makes complete sense in this context. The anxiety-alcohol coping loop documented in your medical record is also consistent — alcohol temporarily suppresses the heightened dopamine state, which creates a feedback loop. This variant doesn't cause anxiety, but it lowers the threshold for it under stress.
The upside: Sales, Muay Thai, high-stakes situations — slower dopamine clearance likely gives you a real edge here. The goal isn't to eliminate the variant's effect, it's to manage the downside while keeping the upside.

AMPD1 — the muscle energy carrier
Worth knowing
One copy of the variant · rs17602729 · carrier status only (autosomal recessive)
The simple version: During hard exercise, your muscles burn through ATP — their fuel — and need to replenish it fast. AMPD1 helps manage that replenishment. Your version of this gene has a loss-of-function mutation.
What carrier status means: You have one copy, not two. Because this condition is autosomal recessive — two copies required to fully express it — you're a carrier rather than affected. Most carriers are completely asymptomatic. The effect at one copy is subtle at most.
Why it's still worth knowing: If you've ever noticed recovery after a hard Muay Thai session or heavy lift feels disproportionate to what you did — this is a plausible contributing factor. Creatine is unusually well-targeted at this variant specifically.
Carrier only — not deficientCreatine well-targeted herePost-training nutrition matters
What to do: Protein and carbs within 60 minutes of every hard session. Creatine 5g daily provides an immediate ATP buffer in muscle cells — the exact gap this variant creates. Magnesium CitraMate in the morning supports the energy pathway.
NAT2 — the slow detoxifier
Clinically significant
Homozygous on both tested variants · rs1799930 + rs1041983 · full slow-acetylator phenotype
The simple version: Your liver uses a process called acetylation to neutralize certain chemicals — including some medications and compounds produced when meat is cooked at high heat. NAT2 runs this process. Your version runs significantly more slowly than average.
What's different about yours: You're homozygous on both tested NAT2 variants — meaning you inherited slow-acetylator alleles from both parents at both positions. This is the full slow-acetylator phenotype. Certain chemicals that enter your body linger longer before being cleared.
Why this matters for medications: Isoniazid (TB treatment), some sulfonamide antibiotics, dapsone, hydralazine, and certain chemotherapy agents all require dose adjustment for slow acetylators — the standard dose can build up to toxic levels. This is well-established and any prescribing doctor should know.
Why it compounds your liver picture: Combined with PNPLA3, your liver is managing two separate vulnerabilities simultaneously. This makes your liver-support protocol — sobriety, NAC, sulforaphane — more important, not optional.
The charred meat connection: Carcinogens formed when meat is charred are metabolized through the NAT2 pathway. Slow acetylators have prolonged exposure to these compounds. It's not a reason to stop grilling — it's a reason not to incinerate your food.

§11

Lifestyle pillars

The non-supplement, non-exercise inputs. What actually produces long-term change when done consistently.

I.

Alcohol — the master lever

Single most leveraged variable on this document. Every flagged biomarker improves when alcohol is out. PNPLA3 means your body pays a premium per drink. Sobriety through June 11 is the floor; extending it is the protocol.

II.

Breathwork Huberman

Box breathing (4-4-4-4) pre-stressful situations. Physiological sigh (double inhale, long exhale) for acute stress. 2 minutes. Direct vagal brake activation — the fastest way to interrupt the COMT-driven dopamine lingering that your anxiety runs on. Build into morning routine before cold calls.

III.

Cold exposure Huberman

11 total minutes per week in 2–4 sessions. Cold enough to want to get out, safe to stay in. 2.5x dopamine increase lasting hours — produces a stable dopamine state that works with your COMT variant rather than against it. Cold shower finish is the lowest-barrier entry.

IV.

Sauna Patrick · Huberman

20 min at 174°F+, 3–4x/week. Cardiovascular benefit approaches drug-level effects at this frequency. Growth hormone pulse supports training recovery. Finnish cohort: 4x/week linked to 66% reduced Alzheimer's risk — relevant given family history gap.

Stress regulation

Your COMT variant and GAD diagnosis make stress regulation structural, not optional. Muay Thai is the primary dopamine regulation tool — exercise is the most effective natural intervention for this specific variant. Breathwork for acute moments. Journaling, Friday dinner tradition, faith community, outdoors — these aren't soft, they're structural.

Therapy continuity

Single referral 2023, no follow-up documented. Given your COMT variant and anxiety-alcohol coping loop, monthly therapy is maintenance infrastructure — not remedial care. Same logic as a monthly trainer session.

VII.

Sunlight

10–20 min within an hour of waking, no sunglasses. 10 min midday if accessible. Circadian regulation, mood, Vitamin D synthesis. Austin makes this easy year-round — no barrier to entry.

VIII.

Environmental toxin reduction

Per PCP's specific advice: EWG Skin Deep database for product checks. Fragrance-free detergent, mineral (zinc oxide) sunscreen, stainless/glass cookware. Family lung cancer history adds real weight here.

Connection

Friday dinner with 17-year friends. Family. Faith community. Social connection quality predicts all-cause mortality more strongly than most clinical interventions in longitudinal data. Show up consistently.

If using a sleep wearable, morning HRV is the daily readout of autonomic nervous system recovery. Trending down consistently = back off training, increase sleep, audit stressors. Not vanity — it's the dashboard warning light.
