Genetic blueprint

Variants that affect your day-to-day health choices. Pharmacogenomic variants for drugs you're not taking and variants with no clinical consequence are omitted.

Verified directly from raw AncestryDNA file (677,436 SNPs · build 37.1 · Apr 23, 2026)

MTHFR C677TOne copy · rs1801133 · A/G
Your B-vitamin processing enzyme runs at reduced speed.

Think of MTHFR as a converter that turns the folate in food into the form your body can actually use. Your version of this enzyme works at roughly 65% efficiency. The downstream effect is that certain critical processes — cardiovascular health, mood regulation, gut lining integrity, and cellular repair — get less of what they need. This is the primary explanation for your elevated homocysteine: it's a waste product that builds up when this conversion is sluggish. Without MTRR compounding it (that variant is not present in your DNA), MTHFR alone is manageable with the right B-vitamin form.

What to do: Take B vitamins in their pre-converted "methylated" form — Methyl-Guard Plus does this. Avoid supplements listing "folic acid" — that's the unprocessed form your enzyme struggles with. Target homocysteine below 7 µmol/L (currently 8.4 and trending down — good).
CFH Y402HOne copy · rs1061170 · T/C
Your eyes' inflammation control system is slightly less effective.

Your eyes have a built-in system that manages inflammation and protects the retina. This variant makes that system less efficient, which over decades can allow damage to accumulate in the macula — the part of the eye responsible for sharp central vision. It won't affect you noticeably for decades, but the interventions that prevent it work best when started early. Texas sun exposure and your outdoor lifestyle make this genuinely worth managing proactively.

What to do: Annual dilated eye exam — ophthalmologist, not just optometrist. UV-blocking sunglasses outdoors always. Never smoke. The omega-3s you're already taking directly support retinal health.
PNPLA3One copy · rs738409 · C/G
Your liver is genetically more vulnerable to fat buildup and alcohol damage.

This gene helps your liver clear fat from its own cells. Your version is less efficient at this, meaning fat accumulates more easily — particularly with alcohol or high refined carbohydrate intake. This is the most direct genetic explanation for your three episodes of elevated AST/ALT on record. Your liver isn't broken — it just has less margin for error than average. A bad weekend of drinking that someone else shrugs off, your liver registers as a significant event.

What to do: Alcohol is the most harmful input for this variant specifically — your sobriety protocol directly protects your liver. NAC and sulforaphane support your liver's own protective systems. Low refined carbs reduce fat accumulation. ALT/AST at every annual physical.
COMT Val158MetOne copy · rs4680 · A/G
Your brain clears stress chemicals more slowly than average.

After a stressful, exciting, or emotionally intense experience, your brain releases dopamine. Most people clear it relatively quickly and return to baseline. Your version of this gene clears it more slowly — you stay in a heightened state longer. On good days this can be an advantage: sharper focus, stronger drive, higher motivation under pressure. Under sustained load it means stress accumulates faster, anxiety lingers longer, and stimulants like caffeine hit harder and last longer. This is directly relevant to your GAD diagnosis and the anxiety-alcohol coping pattern in your record.

What to do: Hard caffeine cutoff before 11am — not 2pm. Caffeine's half-life is 5–7 hours, and your slower clearance extends that further. Exercise (Muay Thai) is the most effective natural dopamine regulator for this variant. Box breathing (4-4-4-4) before stressful situations — sales calls, difficult conversations — directly activates the calming branch of your nervous system. Magnesium glycinate before bed helps wind the elevated state down at night.
AMPD1One copy · rs17602729 · A/G
Your muscles may fatigue faster during intense exercise — carrier status only.

During hard exercise, your muscles burn through ATP — their fuel — and need to replenish it quickly. This gene manages that replenishment process. You carry one copy of a loss-of-function variant, which means you're a carrier for AMPD1 deficiency — not homozygous deficient. At carrier status most people are asymptomatic or notice only subtle effects. It's worth knowing given Muay Thai twice weekly plus lifting, but the effect at one copy is modest compared to two copies.

What to do: Post-training protein and carbs within 60 minutes of every hard session. Creatine provides an immediate ATP buffer in muscle cells — directly relevant here. Magnesium CitraMate in the morning supports the energy pathway. If you notice recovery feels disproportionately slow, it's worth mentioning to a sports medicine doctor.
NAT2 (slow acetylator)Two copies · rs1799930 + rs1041983 · both homozygous
Your body processes certain drugs and dietary toxins significantly more slowly than average.

Your liver uses a process called acetylation to neutralize certain chemicals — including some medications and compounds produced when meat is cooked at high heat. You have two copies of slow-acetylator variants across two separate NAT2 SNPs, both homozygous. This is the full slow-acetylator phenotype — not partial. It means certain drugs stay in your system longer than standard dosing assumes, and that charred-meat carcinogens linger before being cleared. This is also relevant context for your liver picture alongside the PNPLA3 variant — your liver is carrying multiple challenges simultaneously.

What to do: Tell any prescribing doctor you are a "NAT2 slow acetylator" — particularly relevant for isoniazid (TB medication), certain sulfonamides, and some chemotherapy agents where dose adjustment is standard practice. Avoid charred or heavily well-done meat as a consistent habit. Sulforaphane from broccoli sprouts directly activates the detox pathways this gene is supposed to run. This is a stronger finding than previously stated — worth taking seriously.
VKORC1One copy · rs7294 · T/C
Blood thinner medications would require dose adjustment in your body.

If you ever need a blood thinner — warfarin is the most common — your body processes it differently than the standard dose assumes. This variant is well-established in pharmacogenomics and is one of the main reasons warfarin dosing is now routinely tested before prescribing. It doesn't affect your daily life at all right now, but it's a permanent flag for any prescribing physician.

What to do: Note "VKORC1 variant (rs7294, heterozygous)" in your medical file. Mention it to any doctor who prescribes anticoagulants. Nothing to act on today.
LCT / MCM6Two copies · rs4988235 · A/A

Most mammals lose the ability to digest lactose after childhood. You have two copies of the lactase persistence variant — homozygous — which means the enzyme stays active permanently. This is confirmed from the raw file. There is no genetic basis for avoiding dairy.

What to do: Nothing. This is a green light. Greek yogurt, kefir, and cheese are part of your gut protocol — enjoy them without concern.
